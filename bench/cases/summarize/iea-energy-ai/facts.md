# Dense Factual Notes: IEA Report on Energy & AI (Executive Summary)

**Source:** IEA, "Executive Summary: Key Questions on Energy and AI"
**Publication Date:** Post-April 2025 (builds on landmark April 2025 *Energy and AI* report; text references a "70% surge in gas turbine orders in 2025" as a past event, implying a later-2025 or 2026 publication date not explicitly stated).

---

## Investment & Market Dynamics
- **Tech Capex > Oil & Gas:** Capital expenditure of just five technology companies is now larger than global investment in oil and natural gas production. (Source text: "Capital expenditure of just five technology companies is now larger than global investment in oil and natural gas production.")
- **Capex Figures:** Tech company capex exceeded USD 400 billion in 2025 and is expected to jump by another 75% in 2026.
- **AI Factory Capacity:** IEA satellite-based tracking shows "artificial intelligence (AI) factories" – cutting-edge data centres specifically designed for AI – more than tripled in capacity over the past 18 months.
- **Funding Shift:** Data centre investments have grown too large to be funded from company balance sheets alone; large amounts of funding from capital markets will be critical. Pace of growth sensitive to market sentiment and expectations for returns.
- **Venture Capital:** The almost two-fold jump in venture capital going into AI-enabled robotics/automation in 2025 is cited as a marker of industrial innovation potential.

## Energy Efficiency vs. Use-Case Intensity
- **Efficiency Rate:** "Software and hardware advances have resulted in the energy use per AI task dropping by at least an order of magnitude annually in recent years."
- **Simple Query Consumption:** Simple text queries now typically consume less electricity than running a television over the same period. If all conventional internet searches were performed with simple AI text queries, it would consume less than 4 TWh annually (less than 1% of total data centre consumption).
- **Intensive Use Cases:** New applications (video generation, reasoning, agentic tasks) can consume hundreds or thousands of times more energy per query than simple text generation.
- **AI Usage Growth:** Major model providers reported a threefold increase in active users and a fivefold increase in revenue over the past year.

## Data Centre Electricity Demand
- **2025 Growth:** Global electricity demand of data centres grew by 17% in 2025. AI-focused data centre electricity consumption surged 50% in 2025.
- **Central Projection (2030):** Electricity consumption from data centres roughly doubling from 485 TWh in 2025 to ~950 TWh in 2030 (accounting for ~3% of global electricity demand). *Note: A chart note states "Total = 800 TWh," possibly indicating a different scenario or error.* AI-focused data centre consumption triples in this period.
- **Longer-Term Upside:** Investments to relieve bottlenecks and rapid uptake of energy-intensive AI use cases raise the prospect of an even higher upside case after 2030.
- **Emissions:** Emissions associated with data centres double in IEA projections, reaching around 350 million tonnes in 2035 (about 2% of global electricity sector emissions by that date).

## Power Density & Infrastructure Challenges
- **Server Rack Density:** An advanced data centre server rack (size of a large refrigerator) could have peak power demand equivalent to 65 households by 2027.
- **Density Increase:** AI server power density increased 11x between 2020–2025; a further fourfold increase is set by 2027.
- **Power Swings:** AI training and model use induce large, rapid power swings, making energy storage critical.
- **Battery Storage:** By 2030, around 20–25 GW of battery storage could be installed in data centres globally. A data centre operator recently signed an agreement for the largest battery project ever by energy capacity (four times larger than the previous record-holder), helping commercialize long-duration energy storage.
- **Supply Chain Bottlenecks:** A shortage of high-bandwidth memory (integral to AI chip production) has developed over the past six months and is anticipated to persist through at least end-2027. Critical inputs for power electronics and transformers rely on a small number of producers, notably China.

## Onsite Natural Gas Generation
- **U.S. Activity:** In the United States, data centre developers push projects with onsite natural gas-based power generation. IEA satellite tracking indicates ~one-fifth have started land clearing or construction.
- **Overbuilding Requirement:** New IEA analysis shows providing reliable onsite gas-fired electricity requires overbuilding onsite generation infrastructure by 30–70% relative to demand.
- **2030 Projection:** Around 15–27 GW of onsite natural gas may power data centres by 2030, mostly in the United States. (High uncertainty noted).
- **Grid Preference:** Most data centres still prefer connecting to the grid; onsite gas doesn't remove the urgency of addressing grid bottlenecks.
- **Turbine Chokepoint:** A 70% surge in gas turbine orders in 2025 has highlighted chokepoints in energy technology supply chains.

## Macroeconomic & Energy Demand Linkage
- **AI-Driven GDP Boost:** First-of-its-kind IEA-OECD cooperation analysis shows AI-driven economic growth is concentrated in knowledge-intensive services and high-income countries, where the elasticity between energy demand and activity is lower.
- **Demand Impact:** An AI-driven growth boost could raise the level of global energy demand by 1–4% in 2035 compared with no-AI-boost trends. Effects concentrated in advanced economies (particularly the United States).
- **Policy Dominance:** The impact of energy policies and energy technology developments on energy demand is much larger than an AI-driven economic growth boost.

## Energy Security, Sustainability & Industrial Impact
- **Conflict & Infrastructure:** Data centres have been targeted in conflict zones, underscoring their role as critical infrastructure. Trade restrictions have targeted components like critical minerals for power electronics and batteries.
- **Potential Savings:** Well-documented AI use cases have the potential to save over 13 exajoules (EJ) of energy by 2035, equivalent to 3% of global final energy consumption (if barriers are overcome). AI-enabled process optimization could reduce energy costs by 3–10 percentage points in energy-intensive industries.
- **AI Race:** China is particularly focused on the race to apply AI across the economy for innovation and productivity.

## Barriers to AI Adoption in Energy
- **Top Barrier:** An IEA survey of energy companies reveals lack of digital skills is the single largest barrier.
- **Data & Policy Gaps:** Only 10% of global electricity consumption is covered by open electricity data policies. Less than half of energy demand is covered by policy frameworks promoting AI uptake. Fragmented data, data protection, privacy, and cybersecurity concerns are also limiting factors.
- **Digitalization Gap:** Inadequate digitalization of equipment is noted as a barrier.

## Social & Affordability Concerns
- **Price Impact Context:** Impact on electricity prices depends on supply-demand balances. Predictable baseload demand can lower prices; tight systems with new investment needs can raise prices. Data centres' rapid, large footprint creates risks of misalignment and potential price increases "if unmitigated."
- **Public Sentiment:** Surveys show citizens are concerned about AI's effect on jobs, the economy, the environment, and society. Energy prices are a flashpoint post-Covid-19 and the global energy crisis.

## IEA's Three Proposed Policy Principles
1.  Proactive management of data centre pipelines and electricity sector investment (reforming connection queues, robust demand projections via disclosure and cooperation, fair cost allocation).
2.  Promote electricity system flexibility (non-firm grid connections, demand response incentives, grid-interactive onsite assets like batteries and generators turning data centres from loads to resources).
3.  Remove barriers to AI adoption in energy (comprehensive policy on data, cybersecurity, skills, interoperability; demonstrate AI benefits for affordable, secure, sustainable energy).
