## Factual Notes from Microsoft Cloud Blog Post (July 25, 2024)

**Source:** `https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-cloud/blog/2024/07/25/sustainable-by-design-transforming-datacenter-water-efficiency/`  
**Title:** Sustainable by design: Transforming datacenter water efficiency  
**Author:** Noelle Walsh, Corporate Vice President, Cloud Operations & Innovation, Microsoft  
**Publication Date:** July 25, 2024

- Microsoft unveiled its **Datacenter Community Pledge** “last month” (June 2024), framing datacenters as a “force for good” and committing to freshwater protection and replenishment in operating regions and worldwide.  
- The company has a goal to become **water positive by 2030**.  
- Water intensity (water consumed per kilowatt-hour) has been reduced **by over 80%** from its first generation of owned datacenters (early 2000s) to its current generation in 2023.  
- Two focus areas for further reducing water intensity:  
  1. conserving water at every stage of operations, and  
  2. innovative technologies that reduce water needed for cooling.  
- Datacenters are operated at a temperature that allows cooling with outdoor air for the majority of the year.  
- A **2022 audit** resulted in targeted improvements that **eliminated 90% of instances where excess water was used**.   
- Advanced prediction models are used to anticipate water requirements based on real-time weather and operational data; discrepancies with actual consumption help identify leaks and inefficiencies.  
- Water conservation strategies are tailored to bioregion. Examples:  
  - **Texas, Washington, California, Singapore**: expanded use of reclaimed and recycled water.  
  - **Netherlands, Ireland, Sweden**: harvesting rainwater.  
  - Rainwater harvesting is being brought to new datacenters in **Canada, the United Kingdom, Finland, Italy, South Africa, and Austria**.  
- **Cold plates** are a direct-to-chip cooling technology: closed-loop system that dissipates heat effectively, “…like a car radiator.” A “sidekick” liquid cooling system is already used adjacent to racks of **Microsoft Azure Maia AI Accelerator** chips.  
- **Microfluidics**: integrating tiny fluid channels into chip designs, bringing liquid cooling inside the silicon next to processors.  
- Newest datacenter designs optimized for AI workloads **consume zero water for cooling**, using chip-level cooling without evaporation.  
- Water positive strategy pillars: reduction, replenishment, access, innovation, policy.  
- **Water replenishment program**: Nearly doubled portfolio to **more than 49 replenishment projects** globally; combined potential to replenish **more than 24,000 Olympic-size swimming pools** over project lifetimes.  
- **Water access target met** ahead of 2030: provided **1.5 million people** with access to clean water and sanitation services.  
- Advocacy activities:  
  - Serving on a coalition to increase water reuse and recycling across the **United States**.  
  - Funding projects that support Tribal Nations and state governments in increasing water security.  
  - Supporting research, analysis, and advocacy on water in the **European Commission**.  
- Referenced report: “Why investment in water is crucial to tackling the climate crisis,” **World Economic Forum, 2024**.  
- Microsoft’s own **2024 Environmental Sustainability Report** is cited for further progress details.  
- The blog post is part of the “Sustainable by design” blog series; other posts mentioned include:  
  - “Sustainable by design: Advancing the sustainability of AI”  
  - “Sustainable by design: Innovating for energy efficiency in AI, part 1”  
  - “Sustainable by design: Innovating for energy efficiency in AI, part 2”  
  - “Sustainable by design: Advancing low carbon materials”  
  - “Sustainable by design: Next-generation datacenters consume zero water for cooling”  
  - “Sustainable by design: Innovating for zero waste”  

**Potential flags:** The post is promotional in tone (Microsoft marketing its own sustainability efforts). Claims such as “over 80% reduction” and “zero water for cooling” are company-asserted and may be difficult to independently verify. No third-party validation is provided.
