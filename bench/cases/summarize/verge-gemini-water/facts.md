## Factual Notes from Source

**Source:** The Verge, article by Justine Calma  
**Title:** "Google says a typical AI text prompt only uses 5 drops of water — experts say that’s misleading"  
**Publication Date:** August 21, 2025  
**URL:** https://www.theverge.com/report/763080/google-ai-gemini-water-energy-emissions-study

### Google’s Claimed Metrics (from its own study, not peer-reviewed)
- **Water:** Median Gemini text prompt uses ~5 drops of water, or 0.26 milliliters.
- **Electricity:** ~0.24 watt-hours (Wh) per median prompt, equated to watching TV for less than nine seconds.
- **Carbon emissions:** ~0.03 grams of CO₂ per median prompt.
- **Efficiency gains:** From May 2024 to May 2025, Google claims a 33x reduction in electricity per prompt and a 44x reduction in carbon footprint per median prompt.

### Expert Critiques & Identified Omissions
- **Indirect water use omitted:** Google’s water figure only covers direct data center cooling. It excludes water consumed off-site for electricity generation (cooling at power plants, steam turbines), which experts say is the majority of a data center’s total water footprint.  
    > “You only see the tip of the iceberg, basically.” — Alex de Vries-Gao, founder of Digiconomist, PhD candidate at Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam.  
- **Carbon metric is market-based, not location-based:** Google shares only a “market-based” emissions figure, reflecting renewable energy commitments. A “location-based” measure, showing actual grid mix where the data center operates, is withheld. Experts note location-based emissions are typically higher and represent “groundtruth.”  
    > “They’re just hiding the critical information.” — Shaolei Ren, associate professor of electrical and computer engineering, UC Riverside.  
- **Methodology differences obscure comparisons:** Google’s study uses a median prompt (to avoid outlier skew), whereas earlier research by Ren and de Vries-Gao used averages. Google did not disclose the word count or token length defining its median prompt.  
- **Misleading comparison with earlier research:** Google says its 0.26 ml figure is “orders of magnitude less than previous estimates” of up to 50 ml. Ren states his earlier co-authored paper included total direct and indirect water consumption, making the comparison invalid.

### Broader Context from Google’s Own Disclosures
- **Overall emissions rising:** Google’s “ambitions-based carbon emissions” grew 11% in 2024 and 51% since 2019, per its latest sustainability report.  
- **Emissions categories excluded:** Google recently began excluding “peripheral” greenhouse gas emissions categories from its climate goals, deeming them outside its direct control.  
- **Jevons paradox flagged:** Efficiency gains per prompt may be offset by overall growth in AI usage, leading to higher total resource consumption and pollution.

### Study Status
- Google has not submitted the paper for peer review; a spokesperson said they are open to doing so in the future. The company declined to answer a list of detailed questions from The Verge on the record.

### Key Statements from Google Representatives
- Amin Vahdat (VP/GM of AI & Infrastructure, Google Cloud) and Jeff Dean (Chief Scientist, Google DeepMind and Google Research), in a blog: “While we’re proud of the innovation behind our efficiency gains so far, we’re committed to continuing substantial improvements in the years ahead.”
